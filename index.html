The process of vision begins with the presence of external energy in the form of light. Light is a narrow band of electromagnetic radiation that can be conceptualized as a wave or a stream of photons. It consists of different wavelengths, and when it enters our eyes, it acts as the physical stimulus for vision. Not all light is visible to the human eye; visible light represents only a small portion of the electromagnetic spectrum.
The light enters the eye through the cornea. It then passes through the aqueous humor, the iris, the pupil (the dark circular opening at the center of the iris), and the lens. The lens helps focus light on the retina by changing its shape. The retina is a light-sensitive membrane in the back of the eye that contains photoreceptors (rods and cones) responsible for transducing light energy into neural signals. Photoreceptors, which are cells in the retina, convert light energy into neural energy. The retina also includes the fovea, the area with the highest acuity, and the optic disc, where retinal ganglion cells leave the eye via the optic nerve (a bundle of nerve fibers), and where blood vessels enter the eye. The electrical signals generated by the photoreceptors are processed by other retinal cells and then sent through the optic nerve from each eye.
The optic nerves carry visual signals towards the brain. Before reaching the brain, some fibers of the optic nerve cross over at the optic chiasm, integrating visual information from both eyes. From there, the visual information is relayed to various brain areas for processing and interpretation. The optic nerve fibers synapse at the Lateral Geniculate Nucleus (LGN) in the thalamus, acting as a relay station that filters and processes visual information. Neural signals from the left visual field go to the right hemisphere of the brain, and signals from the right visual field go to the left hemisphere. The LGN has different layers, including Parvocellular layers (top four layers) carrying information about fine spatial details from P ganglion cells, and Magnocellular layers (bottom two layers) carrying information about dynamic visual properties of motion from M ganglion cells.
The visual information from the LGN goes to the primary visual cortex (V1) also known as the striate cortex, in the occipital lobe. It plays a crucial role in early visual processing, such as detecting edges, orientations, and simple features in the visual scene. From V1, the visual information is relayed to higher visual association areas, such as V2, V3, and beyond. These areas process more complex visual features, combining information from different parts of the visual field. The inferior temporal cortex is involved in object recognition. It processes complex visual stimuli, allowing the recognition and identification of objects, faces, and other visual patterns.

Interpreting visual information involves two processes: bottom-up and top-down processing. Bottom-up processing analyzes individual features and elements, recognizing them as objects. For example, when looking at a face, we identify eyes, nose, and mouth to recognize the face. Top-down processing is driven by memory, knowledge, and expectations. It helps us interpret visual information based on past experiences, allowing recognition of familiar objects even with incomplete visual data.

Combining bottom-up and top-down processing enables our brain to understand and recognize objects and scenes in the visual world. This continuous process occurs as we interact with our environment and make sense of visual stimuli.
